## The Excitement

Sarah arrived at the coffee shop already on her phone.

"I'm doing it," she said, slipping into the booth. "I'm finally pulling the trigger."

Marcus lowered his newspaper. "Pulling the trigger on what?"

"NVIDIA. NVDA. I'm putting four thousand dollars in this afternoon."

Elena looked up from her latte. "Why now?"

"Because forty-seven Wall Street analysts rate it a Buy. Average target: two-fifteen. Stock's at one-eighty. That's nineteen percent upside, just sitting there." She slid her phone across the table. "Look. *Forty-seven* analysts. They can't all be wrong."

Marcus glanced at the screen. "I read something this morning. Some Benjamin Graham guy's framework. Said NVDA was overvalued. *Sell.*"

"That's the part I don't get." Sarah threw her hands up. "Same stock. Same week. One says Buy, one says Sell. It's nonsense. One of them has to be wrong."

"Or both of them are right."

The voice came from behind her. Alex set down his coffee and pulled up a chair.

"Both?" Sarah turned. "How can two opposite calls both be right?"

"Let me tell you about a traveler I met once."

---

## The Two Maps

Alex stirred his coffee.

"She was standing at a crossroads. Holding two maps. Both drawn by experts. Both showed how to get to the same city. But one map said *turn left.* The other said *turn right.*"

Elena leaned in. "So one of them was wrong?"

"That's what she thought. So she went back to the first cartographer. Asked him which one was correct. He shrugged. *'I drew that for travelers who want to arrive by sunset. Take the highway. It costs more in tolls — but you'll be there in two hours.'*"

Alex took a slow sip.

"Then she went to the second cartographer. Showed her the other map. *'I drew that for travelers who want to arrive cheaply. Take the back roads. It'll take you all day — but you won't pay a cent.'*"

Marcus set down his newspaper. "So the maps weren't in conflict."

"They were drawn for different journeys."

Silence at the table.

"The traveler didn't have a problem with the maps," Alex said. "She had a problem because she didn't know which journey *she* was on."

Sarah picked up her phone again, slowly this time. "You're saying the analyst rating and the Graham rating…"

"Are two maps. Drawn by experts. Pointing in opposite directions. Both correct."

---

## The Objection

Marcus folded his arms. "Okay, but I've trusted analyst ratings my whole life. Why would I second-guess forty-seven professionals?"

"You shouldn't second-guess them," Alex said. "Trust them — for what they're answering. Wall Street analysts are paid to forecast where the price will be in twelve months. They're often right about that. The problem isn't the analysts. The problem is most people are not, in fact, twelve-month traders. They want to compound their savings over a decade. That's a different journey. Different map."

He turned the laptop back toward Sarah.

"You don't have to take the framework's word over Wall Street's. You just have to know which question is yours."

Sarah stared at the screen for a long moment.

"I'm not a trader. I have a four-thousand-dollar emergency fund I want to grow over twenty years."

"Then which map is yours?"

She closed the NVDA tab.

## The Lesson

Alex closed the laptop.

"Wall Street and Graham aren't in a fight. They're in different zip codes. The Intelligence Era didn't make their disagreement go away. It made it *legible.* You can finally see both maps, side by side, with the journey each one was drawn for."

Elena spoke for the first time in a while.

"So we don't pick *between* the experts. We pick the *expert who's answering our question.*"

"That's the discipline," Alex said. "Wealth without wisdom is luck. Wisdom without wealth is theory. The only way to get both is to know which journey you're on — and then read the map that was drawn for it."

Sarah put her phone face down on the table.

"I think I want to look at four more stocks tonight. Not buy them. Just see both maps for each one."

Alex smiled. "That's how it starts."
