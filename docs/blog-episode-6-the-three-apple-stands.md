A man walks down a country road and sees three apple stands.

At the first stand, the farmer leans on his crate and calls out: *"Best apples in the county."*
At the second: *"Freshest apples in the county."*
At the third: *"Cheapest apples in the county."*

The man stops. He looks at the apples on each stand. They're identical. Same deep red, same size, same shine. He picks one up from each stand and squeezes them. They feel the same. He smells them. They smell the same.

He stands there for a long time.

Then he does what most people do when they can't decide. He picks the cheapest one — because at least he knows he didn't get robbed. He pays his dollar and walks away.

Two hours later, he bites into the apple and spits it out. It's mealy. Five days old. The "cheapest" was cheap because the farmer was trying to unload old fruit. If the man had walked back to the second stand — *"Freshest apples in the county"* — he'd have spent two dollars on an apple worth eating.

He had the data. He could see all three stands. He could even *touch* the apples. What he didn't have was a way to compare them on the dimension that mattered. So he defaulted to the dimension he could see: price.

**The eye lies.** And in investing, so does the spreadsheet.

---

### This is what happens in "the AI boom"

Marcus is 38. He has put 15 years of paychecks into a 401k he never thinks about. Now he has $10,000 sitting in a brokerage account because his cousin keeps saying *"You have to get into AI before it's too late."*

So Marcus opens his broker app and pulls up three stocks: **NVDA**, **AMD**, **AVGO**. Three semiconductor companies. All ride the AI boom. All look great in the charts. All have analyst ratings of *Buy*. All have green numbers everywhere he looks.

He needs to pick one. Maybe two. He has $10,000 and no time to research three companies properly.

Marcus does what most people with a software background do. He opens Excel.

For six hours on a Saturday, he builds a spreadsheet. He copies P/E ratios from Yahoo. He copies revenue growth, market cap, profit margin, debt-to-equity. He builds a column for each stock and a row for each metric. By dinnertime he has a beautiful grid of numbers.

He stares at it.

NVDA has the highest revenue growth — *but* the highest P/E. AMD has the lowest P/E — *but* mediocre margins. AVGO has the best margins — *but* boring revenue growth. He doesn't know which to weight more. He doesn't know what "good" looks like for any of these numbers. He just has data.

After all that work, he defaults to the only dimension he can see clearly. He picks AMD because it's the *cheapest* of the three — lowest P/E ratio. He doesn't want to "overpay." He buys $5,000 worth.

Six months later, here's where the three stocks are:

- AMD: up 5%
- NVDA: up 60%
- AVGO: up 40%

Marcus made $250. He left $3,750 on the table by picking AMD over AVGO.

The spreadsheet didn't lie. It just didn't tell him AVGO's high margins were durable, that NVDA's premium was earned by AI training demand, that AMD's cheapness was a *symptom* not a *bargain.*

**Marcus had data. He didn't have judgment.**

---

### Why "I'll just compare them in a spreadsheet" is the most expensive idea in investing

Here's the thing about a spreadsheet. It does the same thing the man on the road did when he picked up each apple.

It looks at each stock *individually.* It tabulates numbers. It shows you a P/E and an FCF Yield and a debt ratio. But it doesn't tell you which of those numbers *matter most for this kind of company in this kind of market right now.* It doesn't tell you that NVDA's 78x P/E is justified by 200% revenue growth, or that AMD's 28x P/E is *not* justified by 5% revenue growth. It hands you the math and walks away.

A spreadsheet is the modern version of squeezing each apple. It feels rigorous. It produces precise numbers to four decimal places. But precision isn't the same as judgment. Marcus's spreadsheet was precisely wrong.

The discipline of investing isn't in the data. **It's in knowing which numbers to weight, what "good" looks like, and what verdict the data supports.** That kind of judgment used to take a decade to develop, or an MBA in finance, or 600 hours with *The Intelligent Investor* and a yellow highlighter. For most people, that price was too high — so they never built the judgment, and they bought AMD because it was cheapest.

### "But I can build my own model"

Some readers are nodding along thinking *yes, but I'd build a better spreadsheet than Marcus.* Maybe. But the question isn't whether you *could* — it's whether you *will,* every Sunday afternoon, for every stock you ever consider, for the rest of your investing life.

A real comparative model — one that knows what to weight, accounts for sector context, and produces a defensible verdict — takes a CFA candidate two years to build the *judgment* to assemble in their head. After that, applying it to a single 3-stock comparison takes a couple of focused hours.

You're not going to do that. Almost no one does. So you'll do what Marcus did: a half-baked spreadsheet that produces precise numbers without the wisdom to interpret them, and you'll default to the dimension you can see — usually price.
