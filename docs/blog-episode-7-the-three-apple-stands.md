## The Spreadsheet

Marcus pulled out his laptop and slid it across the table.

"Six hours, Saturday afternoon. Look."

Sarah leaned over. The screen showed a beautiful three-column spreadsheet: NVDA, AMD, AVGO. Rows for P/E, revenue growth, profit margin, market cap, debt-to-equity, free cash flow yield.

"Wow," Elena said. "That's organized."

"My nephew kept telling me to get into AI stocks. So instead of just buying what he said, I did it right. I researched. I built a comparison."

"Which one are you buying?" Sarah asked.

Marcus rubbed the back of his neck. "That's the problem. They all look… *fine.* NVDA has the highest growth but the most expensive P/E. AMD's got the lowest P/E but mediocre margins. AVGO's got the best margins but slow revenue growth. So I'm leaning AMD. Lowest P/E. Don't want to overpay."

Alex, who had been listening quietly, set down his cup.

"Marcus, can I tell you about three apple stands?"

---

## The Three Apple Stands

"A man walks down a country road. Sees three apple stands."

Alex held up three fingers.

"First farmer calls out: *'Best apples in the county.'* Second farmer: *'Freshest apples in the county.'* Third: *'Cheapest apples in the county.'*"

He folded his thumb down.

"The man stops. Looks at all three stands. The apples look identical. Same red. Same shine. Same size. He picks one up from each. Squeezes them. Smells them. They feel and smell the same."

"How does he choose?" Elena asked.

"He defaults to the only thing he can see clearly. *Price.* He pays a dollar for the cheapest one, walks away feeling smart. Two hours later, he bites into it. Mealy. Five days old. The 'cheapest' was cheap because the farmer was unloading old fruit."

Marcus looked at his spreadsheet.

"Wait."

"He had the data," Alex said. "He could see all three stands. He could even *touch* the apples. What he didn't have was a way to compare them on the dimension that mattered."

## The Objection

Marcus closed his spreadsheet slowly.

"Okay. But couldn't I have built a better model? Added the ratios I was missing? Learned to interpret them?"

"You could have," Alex said. "Will you?"

Marcus didn't answer.

"A real comparative model — one that knows what to weight, accounts for sector context, and produces a defensible verdict — takes a CFA candidate two years to develop the *judgment* to assemble in their head. After that, applying it to a single three-stock comparison takes a couple of focused hours."

Elena: "Two years to learn. Hours per comparison."

"And almost no one does it. So they default to what Marcus did — beautiful spreadsheet, no judgment, picks the dimension they can see, which is usually price."

Alex tapped the screen.

"Five dollars. Thirty seconds."

## The Lesson

Sarah was quiet for a moment, then spoke.

"This is what I was getting wrong with NVDA last week, isn't it? The data was there. I just didn't have the judgment to interpret it."

"That's the thing about the Intelligence Era," Alex said. "Information was never the bottleneck. Yahoo Finance has been free for twenty years. The bottleneck was always *interpretation.* Knowing which numbers to weight. Knowing what 'good' looks like. Knowing which stock wins on the metrics that actually matter for the kind of investor you want to be."

"You don't have to become a CFA. You just have to read what's in front of you."

"I'm buying AVGO tonight."

The lesson isn't "don't use spreadsheets." It's "don't confuse them with judgment." If your spreadsheet doesn't end with a *verdict* — including the reasoning — you've done math, not analysis.
